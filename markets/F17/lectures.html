9/1: Selfish Routing with applications to Waze

A microcosm of the course featuring routing games. How do people decide what route to drive to work in the morning, and how do their choices impact others? How much time do we waste in traffic, and what can we do to mitigate that?


Required Reading:

  • Easley and Kleinberg, Chapter 8 of Networks, Crowds, and Markets
  • Vickrey, Pricing in Urban and Suburban Transport


Optional Reading:

  • Monnot, Benita and Piliouras, How bad is selfish routing in practice?



9/8: Market Equilibria with applications to Uber (notes)

Market-clearing prices for combinatorial valuations, convergence to equilibrium prices.


Required Reading:

  • Easley and Kleinberg, Chapter 10 of Networks, Crowds, and Markets (but you can skip Section 10.5)
  • Lerner, The Economics of Control, Chapter 2 (handout)


Optional Reading:

  • Hsu, Morgenstern, Rogers, Roth, and Vohra, Do Prices Coordinate Markets?



9/15: Stable Matching with applications to OKCupid (notes)

Matching agents to agents with stable mechanisms, implications of large markets and incomplete information.


Required Reading:

  • Karlin and Peres, Chapter 10.1-10.3 of Game Theory, Alive
  • Gale and Shapley, College Admissions and the Stability of Marriage


Optional Reading:

  • Shi, Assortment Planning in School Choice



9/22: Welfare-Maximizing Auctions with applications to Google AdWords (notes)

Welfare-optimal auctions, price of anarchy.


Required Reading:

  • Roughgarden, Chapter 2 and Chapter 7 of 20 Lectures in Algorithmic Game Theory
  • Vickrey, Counterspeculation, Auctions, and Competitive Sealed Tenders, sections I-VI


Optional Reading:

  • Milgrom and Segal, Deferred-Acceptance Auctions and Radio Spectrum Reallocation



9/29: Discrete Allocation with applications to School Choice (notes)

Matching agents to objects via random serial dictatorship, Hylland-Zeckhauser, and the eating mechanism.


Required Reading:

  • Hylland and Zeckhauser, The Efficient Allocation of Individuals to Positions, sections I (page 296) through section III up to, and including, the example on page 303
  • Bogomolnaia and Moulin, A New Solution to the Random Assignment Problem, sections 1 and 2
  • Roth, Nobel Prize Lecture


Optional Reading:

  • Budish, The Combinatorial Assignment Problem: Approximate Competitive Equilibrium from Equal Incomes



10/6: Revenue-Maximizing Auctions with applications to eBay (notes)

Incomplete information games, Bayes-Nash equilibrium characterization, optimal auctions.


Required Reading:

  • Hartline, Mechanism Design and Approximation, Chapters 2.3-2.5 and 3.3
  • Bulow and Roberts, The Simple Economics of Optimal Auctions, sections I-III


Optional Reading:

  • Celis, Lewis, Mobius, and Nazerzadeh, Buy-it-now or Take-a-chance: Price Discrimination through Randomized Auction



10/13: Simple Auctions & Pricing with applications to Amazon (notes)

Price discrimination, near-optimal auctions, approximation algorithms for pricing, product selection and bundling.


Required Reading:

  • Roughgarden, Chapter 6 of 20 Lectures in Algorithmic Game Theory
  • Bulow and Klemperer, Auctions Versus Negotiations, introduction plus sections I, IIc, and III
  • Rory Sutherland, Never seen the need for a class system? Take a long-haul flight


Optional Reading:

  • Einav, Farronato, Levin, and Sundaresan, Auctions versus Posted Prices in Online Markets
